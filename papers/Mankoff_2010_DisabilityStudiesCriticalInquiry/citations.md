## Key Citations for Follow-up

1. **Linton (1998). *Claiming Disability: Knowledge and Identity.* NYU Press** [ref 40] — The foundational disability studies text cited most frequently in this paper; defines the field, the social model, and the politics of identity. Essential background for anyone entering this literature.

2. **Mankoff, Fait & Tran (2005). "Is your web page accessible?" CHI'05** [ref 48] — The comparative study of web accessibility evaluation methods (automated tools, designers with/without screen readers, remote blind users). Directly relevant to Pool_2023_AccessibilityMetatesting; provides the original empirical baseline on which later multi-tool ensemble research builds.

3. **Balcazar et al. (1998). "Participatory action research and people with disabilities." *Canadian Journal of Rehabilitation*** [ref 4] — The methodological foundation for participatory AT research. Cited as required reading for AT researchers and students. Relevant to any paper in this collection that claims to involve disabled participants.

4. **Scherer (2000). *Living in the State of Stuck.* Brookline Books** [ref 60] — A disability studies account of how assistive technology actually affects people's lives, from a disabled-person-as-subject perspective. Recommended as required reading for AT teaching.

5. **Shneiderman (2000). "Universal usability." *Communications of the ACM* 43(5)** [ref 62] — The paper that defined "universal usability" as access + usability by all; foundational for the universal design strand of this collection (also appears in Wobbrock_2011).
