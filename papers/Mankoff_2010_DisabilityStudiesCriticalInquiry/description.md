Mankoff, Hayes, and Kasnitz (2010) argue that the assistive technology research community has been largely operating within the medical model of disability — treating disability as an individual deficit to fix — while disability studies has spent decades developing social, cultural, and critical frameworks that reveal richer problems and more effective research directions; the paper reviews core disability studies theory (medical/social/postmodern models, language debates, disability rights history) and presents two case studies showing how these frameworks transformed the authors' own work in autism educational technology and web accessibility evaluation. Key practical outputs include a critique of simulation-based accessibility testing as lacking the "soul" of authentic disabled experience, a recommendation for deep longitudinal participatory design with disabled individuals as equal co-researchers across all phases, and a curated reading list anchoring AT researchers in disability studies literature. For this collection, the paper provides the foundational theoretical vocabulary — ableism, nothing-about-us-without-us, social vs. medical model — that contextualizes why user-centered and participatory methods matter, why small non-representative samples (as documented in Mack_2021) are a structural problem, and why technical accessibility metrics alone are insufficient measures of whether a system actually serves disabled people.
