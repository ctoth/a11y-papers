# Audio Description: Making Useful Maps for Blind and Visually Impaired People

**Authors:** Megan Conway, Brett Oppegaard, and Tuyet Hayes
**Year:** 2020
**Venue:** Technical Communication, vol. 67, no. 2, pp. 68-85
**DOI/URL:** ISSN 0049-3155; https://www.stc.org/techcomm/2020/04/28/audio-description-making-useful-maps-for-blind-and-visually-impaired-people/

## One-Sentence Summary

Provides empirically grounded best-practice guidelines for audio describing printed maps (specifically NPS brochure maps) for blind and visually impaired users, derived from three years of mixed-methods stakeholder research through the UniDescription Project.

## Problem Addressed

Maps are among the most complex static visual media to translate into non-visual forms. Professional audio describers routinely skip maps; blind users often see no purpose in maps because they have never had useful ones; and no established guidelines existed specifically for map audio description. This paper fills that gap through applied research on transmodal translation of NPS brochure maps.

## Key Contributions

- Five empirically derived affinity clusters characterizing the problem space for audio-described maps
- A set of best-practices guidelines for map audio description, structured as a step-by-step describer workflow
- Evidence from 69 stakeholders (blind, visually impaired, and sighted administrators/designers/park staff) across 50+ NPS sites over 3+ years
- The UniDescription Project: open-source web tools, mobile apps, and an online academy (UniD Academy) for training subject-matter experts in audio description
- Demonstration that non-specialist subject-matter experts (park rangers, not professional audio describers) can produce useful map audio descriptions with proper guidelines and training

## Methodology

Mixed-methods qualitative research using grounded theory (Glaser et al., 1968):

1. **Literature review** scanning technical communication journals for Audio Description content (found near-zero prior work in TC journals)
2. **14 open-ended research questions** (Table 1) developed from initial literature scan
3. **Three feedback environments:**
   - Semi-structured interviews (in-person, phone, email) with individual stakeholders
   - Semi-structured small group interviews (no cross-talk)
   - Focus groups of up to 9 people with encouraged discussion
4. **Three Descriptathons** (hackathon-style events, 2016-2017) at NPS sites where park staff created audio descriptions of their brochures with iterative feedback
5. **Grounded theory coding:** deductive + inductive, open + closed coding; affinity clustering of themes
6. **Field testing:** usability tests of audio-described content with blind end users at Yosemite (Nov 2017), Muir Woods (Apr 2018), Cape Cod (Jul 2018), Minute Man (Jul 2018), and Morristown (Jul 2018)

**Participants (Table 3):**
- 21 semi-structured interviews across 4 small-group sessions
- Multiple focus groups at NPS sites nationwide (Nov 2015 through Jul 2018)
- 90 people consulted in focus group settings; 21 also participated in interviews
- Total: 69 unique individuals directly affected by or involved with Audio Description
- Mix: blind users, visually impaired users, sighted NPS staff, media designers, administrators from organizations serving blind community (ACB, Lighthouse for the Blind, Library of Congress)

## Implementation Details

### Best-Practices Guidelines (from Discussion/Conclusion, pp. 82-83)

The paper distills findings into actionable guidelines for map audio description:

1. **Short description first:** Provide an overview of the purpose and general content of the map without overwhelming the user with descriptive detail
2. **Infrastructure for navigation:** Build the UniDescription web platform and UniD Academy as a learning space and production tool for subject-matter experts
3. **The purpose of a map matters:** Maps serve different purposes (wayfinding, immersion in historical context, ecological/topographical information). The purpose determines the description approach -- a wayfinding map vs. a historical map require fundamentally different descriptions
4. **A long description:** Allow blind/VI users to immerse themselves in the map, explore areas of interest, and access the map on par with sighted users. Length should not be artificially constrained
5. **A navigation guide for the long description:** Essential for descriptions running more than a few minutes. Allows users to orient themselves and control what they listen to (and for how long)

### Three-Step Description Workflow (derived from findings)

1. **Identify the artifact's purpose** -- what is the map for?
2. **Provide an initial short description** -- overview for information navigation
3. **Elaborate with a more detailed description** -- the full audio description of the artifact

### UniDescription Project Tools

- **www.unidescription.org** -- web-based production and learning platform
- **UniD Academy** -- online training for subject-matter experts
- **UniD mobile app** -- free iOS/Android app for delivering audio descriptions at NPS sites
- All tools are open-source

## Results Summary

### Five Affinity Clusters

**Cluster A: Audio Description, Especially of Maps, Needs Attention**
- Many blind/VI interviewees were perplexed by the idea of map AD -- "Why would a blind person need a map?"
- Professional AD community routinely skips maps
- A blind Library of Congress curator: "I've never seen a good descriptive of a map before... They just say maps have been omitted"
- Maps perceived as inaccessible and unusable, reinforcing exclusion

**Cluster B: Audio Describers Need Additional Guidance**
- Sighted NPS staff found maps the most difficult media to describe
- Clear need for robust guidelines, good examples as models
- The three-step process (purpose, short description, long description) emerged here
- Critical importance of distinguishing between what is "important" vs. describing without judgment

**Cluster C: Boundary Work Wanted and Needed**
- Fundamental theoretical questions remain unresolved: How long should descriptions be? What should they include?
- Key finding: wayfinding maps vs. non-wayfinding maps (ecological, historical) require different description strategies
- Both short and long descriptions needed -- users have different preferences
- A blind Lighthouse administrator: "Start with the larger point of the map and then let me drill down into the detail"

**Cluster D: Experimentation Desired, Not Heavily Prescriptive Rules**
- Stakeholders want guidelines that are specific enough to be useful but not so prescriptive as to produce formulaic descriptions
- Audio Description seen as having potential as an art form, not just a compliance task
- Too much prescription risks "uninspired description"
- Field testers found too much information was better than too little

**Cluster E: Audio Description as Community Outreach**
- Blind/VI participants overwhelmingly expressed desire for maps that enable independent park navigation
- AD as a mechanism for inclusion and agency: "I feel like my park is really making an effort to reach out to me"
- ACB Facebook post about the Yosemite audio-described map shattered engagement records (125,000+ views)
- AD viewed not just as accessibility compliance but as community building

### Key Qualitative Findings

- Maps fundamentally serve three purposes: 1) orientation and movement, 2) exploration of locations of interest, 3) learning about a place (historical, ecological, etc.)
- These purposes overlap, making description complex
- High-quality AD requires subject-matter expertise AND audio description training -- neither alone suffices
- Pilot Descriptathon map descriptions had mixed results: experienced AD authors produced richer descriptions; novices produced basic alt-text-like content

## Limitations

- Qualitative study -- findings are interpretive, not statistically generalizable
- All NPS-context (park brochure maps) -- may not transfer directly to other map types (transit, urban, digital interactive)
- No controlled comparison of different description strategies (acknowledged as future work)
- Participant pool, while diverse in vision status, is self-selected through NPS/ACB/Lighthouse connections
- The paper does not evaluate the effectiveness of the guidelines themselves -- only documents their development
- Almost no empirical testing of what is foundational received wisdom in the AD field (Cluster C finding)

## Testable Properties

- Audio-described maps structured with short+long descriptions should be preferred by users over single-level descriptions
- Navigation guides should increase user satisfaction for descriptions longer than a few minutes
- Subject-matter expertise + AD training should produce higher-quality descriptions than either alone
- Purpose-aligned descriptions (wayfinding vs. historical) should score higher in user satisfaction than generic descriptions
- Too much information is preferred to too little information by blind/VI map users

## Relevance to Project

This paper directly addresses the audio description and hearing/visual accessibility topic areas. It provides:
- The empirical foundation for map audio description guidelines, complementing Biggs_2025_MapEquivalentPurpose's evaluation framework
- Evidence that audio description (one of the three methods scoring 100% in Biggs et al.'s MEP Framework) requires specific training and guidelines to produce well
- A participatory research methodology involving blind stakeholders as co-designers (aligning with Mankoff_2010_DisabilityStudiesCriticalInquiry's recommendations)
- The UniDescription Project as a concrete open-source implementation of accessible map delivery
