# Paper References

Quick reference for papers in this collection.

## How to Read Papers

Each paper folder contains:
- **notes.md** - Implementation-focused notes extracted from the paper (START HERE)
- **description.md** - Brief summary of the paper's contribution
- **paper.pdf** or similar - The original PDF
- **pngs/** - Page images for large PDFs (use when PDF is too large to read directly)
- **chunks/** - Text chunks for very large papers

**For implementation work**: Always read notes.md first - it contains the specific values, formulas, and parameters you need.

**For large PDFs**: Use the pngs/ folder. ImageMagick is installed for PDF-to-image conversion if needed.

## Mack_2021_AccessibilityResearchSurvey
This paper presents the first comprehensive literature survey of accessibility research at CHI and ASSETS from 1994 to 2019, qualitatively coding 506 papers and quantitatively analyzing 836 papers across 26 years. Key findings include that blind/low-vision users dominate the field (43.5% of papers), sample sizes are small (median 13 disabled participants), and emerging areas like neurodiversity, AR/VR, and game accessibility are growing but underrepresented. As the foundational map of the accessibility research landscape, this paper identifies which disability communities receive the most and least research attention and establishes baseline metrics for study methods, contribution types, and temporal trends essential for prioritizing this collection.

## Lazar_2007_ScreenReaderFrustration
Lazar et al. (2007) present an empirical diary study of 100 blind screen reader users who recorded 308 frustrating web experiences, producing a frequency-ranked taxonomy of accessibility failures with page layout (36 occurrences), screen reader–application conflicts (28), and unlabeled forms (23) as the top three causes. The study found that blind users lost an average of 30.4% of their computer session time to frustrating situations — less than sighted users in prior studies — because blind users tended to seek workarounds rather than rebooting, and the average frustration severity was 6.71 out of 9. For this collection, the paper provides the foundational empirical baseline for screen reader accessibility: a ranked, actionable list of web failures that blind users actually encounter, along with behavioral and emotional data that should inform screen reader design, web authoring guidance, and evaluation method research.

## Krishnavajjala_2024_MotorEase
MotorEase is an automated Android tool (ICSE 2024) that detects four categories of motor-impairment accessibility violations in mobile app UIs — inadequate touch target visual size, missing expanding-section closure mechanisms, inconsistent persistent element placement, and insufficient adjacent icon spacing — using a multimodal combination of computer vision (UIED edge detection, Faster-RCNN icon detection) and uiautomator XML metadata analysis. Evaluated on the MotorCheck benchmark of 555 annotated violations across 1,599 screens from 70 Android apps, MotorEase achieves ~90% average accuracy and ~86% F1, substantially outperforming Google Accessibility Scanner and the Groundhog baseline on their respective violation types. For this collection, it provides concrete, implementable detection thresholds (48px visual touch target, 8px icon gap, 95% MSE persistence similarity), a replicable benchmark construction methodology, and the only known automated approach covering these four previously unaddressed motor-accessibility violation categories.

## Wang_2021_ScientificPDFAccessibility
Wang et al. (2021) audit 11,397 scientific PDFs published 2010–2019 across 19 fields of study and find that only 2.4% satisfy all five standard PDF accessibility criteria, with 74.7% meeting none, establishing that inaccessibility is the near-universal default for scholarly literature. They introduce SciA11y, a machine-learning pipeline (built on S2ORC and DeepFigures) that converts scientific PDFs into accessible HTML with proper heading structure, bidirectional citation links, and a table of contents, processing 12 million papers with 87% of renders having no or only some readability problems, validated through a qualitative user study with six blind and low-vision researchers. For this collection, the paper provides the foundational empirical baseline for scientific PDF accessibility, five concrete design recommendations for accessible document reader systems, and key findings that LaTeX—the dominant tool in CS, physics, and mathematics—produces among the least accessible PDFs.

## Yuan_2011_GameAccessibilitySurvey
This 2011 survey by Yuan, Folmer, and Harris establishes the foundational framework for game accessibility research through a three-step game interaction model (receive stimuli, determine response, provide input) that maps each disability type to exactly the step it disrupts, explaining why fundamentally different solution strategies are needed for visual, hearing, cognitive, and motor impairments. Using 2002 US Census data, the paper estimates that 32.2 million Americans (~11% of the population) have their game-play affected by a disability, with 6.3 million completely excluded, and surveys dozens of accessible games across 8 genres to extract a two-level taxonomy of high- and low-level accessibility strategies. For this collection, it provides the conceptual backbone for all game accessibility work, the definitive strategy taxonomy (Table 7) linking disability-to-step-to-implementation, and population-scale evidence for the scope of game inaccessibility as a research and design problem.

## Gartland_2022_WebAccessibilityCognitive
This 2022 rapid evidence assessment by Gartland et al. synthesizes 45 peer-reviewed studies (2006-2021) on web accessibility for people with cognitive disabilities (PwCDs), finding that text-heavy web content and the exclusion of PwCDs from design processes are the dominant, persistent barriers despite decades of WCAG development. The review identifies three major contribution types across the corpus — barrier documentation, tool development and evaluation, and methodological best practices — and produces a four-quadrant framework (People, Technology, Space, Process) for conducting inclusive research with PwCDs as co-designers rather than test subjects. For this collection, this paper serves as the foundational evidence map for cognitive accessibility, anchoring claims about which WCAG criteria matter for PwCDs, which cognitive disability populations are under-researched, and what research methods produce ecologically valid findings.

## Pool_2023_AccessibilityMetatesting
Pool (2023) empirically compares nine integration-amenable automated web accessibility testing tools (alfa, axe-core, Continuum, Equal Access, HTML CodeSniffer, Nu Html Checker, QualWeb, Tenon, and WAVE) by running all nine against a 121-page corpus, finding that every pairwise tool combination is bidirectionally complementary — adding any second tool increases issue instance discovery by 13–767% and adds 15–51 distinct issue types, with each tool holding at least 7 issues it alone detects. The study maps the tools' 1,327 tests to 245 distinct accessibility issues aligned to WCAG 2.1, documents per-tool specializations (e.g., axe-core for color contrast and landmarks, WAVE for label clarity, Nu Html Checker for attribute validity), and enumerates 14 non-trivial engineering integration costs for multi-tool ensemble deployment. For this collection, the paper is the foundational empirical reference on automated evaluation methods: it quantifies why no single tool suffices, establishes which tools specialize in which issue categories, and frames the ensemble testing trade-off between discovery completeness and integration effort.

## Kafle_2017_CaptionUsabilityDHH
Kafle and Huenerfauth (ASSETS 2017) introduce the Automated-Caption Evaluation (ACE) metric, which replaces Word Error Rate for evaluating ASR caption quality for Deaf and Hard-of-Hearing users by weighting each ASR error according to the word's unpredictability (Shannon entropy from a bidirectional n-gram model) and its semantic deviation from the reference word (word2vec cosine distance), with a tuned alpha of 0.65 favoring predictability. In a user study with 30 DHH participants rating 45 sentence pairs with identical WER but differing ACE scores, ACE-preferred captions received significantly higher usability ratings (Wilcoxon p<0.0001) and correlated with subjective scores at Spearman ρ=0.743 versus WER's ρ=0.109. For this collection, the paper provides the foundational DHH-specific ASR evaluation framework — directly actionable for selecting or training captioning systems — and fills the hearing accessibility gap identified in the Mack 2021 survey.

## Singh_2024_FigurA11y
Singh et al. (2024) present FigurA11y, a human-AI collaborative system for writing scientific alt text that combines a training-free GPT-4 pipeline — conditioned on extracted figure metadata (caption, mentioning paragraphs, EasyOCR text, data tables) and DIAGRAM Center/SIGACCESS accessibility guidelines organized by figure type — with two interactive authoring features: Generate at Cursor (on-demand continuation/infilling at the author's cursor position) and Potential User Questions (auto-generated ambiguity questions with suggested answers, to prompt authors to address coverage gaps). A within-subjects user study (N=14 paper authors describing their own figures) found that generated drafts enabled rapid description initiation and that Interactive Assistance prompted more iterative refinement without increasing cognitive load or manual effort relative to the base Draft+Revise version. For this collection, the paper provides the state-of-the-art implementation blueprint for AI-assisted scientific alt text authoring, including full prompt templates (Appendix B), the metadata extraction pipeline, and a validated design connecting to Wang_2021_ScientificPDFAccessibility's finding that alt text is the most urgent unaddressed gap in scientific document accessibility.


## Wobbrock_2011_AbilityBasedDesign
Wobbrock, Kane, Gajos, Harada, and Froehlich (2011) present ability-based design, a principled accessible computing framework that reframes design from compensating for disability to leveraging what users *can* do, articulated through seven principles (Ability, Accountability, Adaptation, Transparency, Performance, Context, Commodity) organized into Stance, Interface, and System categories. The paper demonstrates the framework through 14 example projects spanning desktop text entry (TrueKeys, Trackball EdgeWrite), mouse pointing (Angle Mouse, SUPPLE), mobile devices (Slide Rule, Walking User Interfaces), and web access (WebAnywhere), each mapped explicitly to the principles it upholds. For this collection, the paper provides the foundational theoretical vocabulary for all adaptive and accessible interface work — the seven principles serve as an evaluative checklist for any assistive or adaptive system, and the shift from "design for all" to "universal application of design-for-one" anchors research across motor impairment, visual impairment, situational impairment, and beyond.


## Martins_2023_LargeScaleWebA11y
Martins and Duarte (2023) conduct an automated WCAG 2.1 accessibility evaluation of 2,884,498 web pages from 166,311 websites (March–September 2021), finding an average of 30 accessibility errors per page with only 0.5% of pages having zero errors, and identifying that the top failures are text contrast (79% of pages), missing accessible names (68%), and improper ARIA roles and properties (24%, via WCAG 1.3.1). Using Wappalyzer and SimilarTech to fingerprint 3,482 technologies across 32 categories, they apply Mann-Whitney U and Kruskal-Wallis tests to show that 29 of 32 technology categories have statistically significant relationships with the A3 accessibility metric, with specific technologies like Isotope.js (A3=0.328), jQuery (0.472), and React (0.613) associated with poor accessibility, while Python (0.816), Mustache JS (0.809), and Jimdo (0.855) correlate with better outcomes. For this collection, the paper provides the largest empirical baseline linking web technology choice to accessibility level, directly implicating improper ARIA usage as a measurable cause of 24% of all web pages failing WCAG 1.3.1, with a publicly available dataset (Zenodo DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.7494722) enabling further ARIA-specific analysis.


## Newell_2000_UserSensitiveInclusiveDesign
Newell and Gregor argue that "Universal Design" and "Design for All" set goals that are unachievable for most HCI products — accessibility for one disability group often conflicts with accessibility for another, and claiming universal coverage discourages designers from attempting inclusive work at all. They propose "User Sensitive Inclusive Design" (USID) as an extension of User Centred Design that acknowledges the extraordinary breadth of disabled user functionality, specifies seven methodological challenges that distinguish inclusive UCD from mainstream UCD, and substitutes "sensitive" for "centred" and "inclusive" for "universal" to reflect what is actually achievable. This paper is the canonical theoretical foundation for framing accessibility research as a distinct paradigm rather than a checklist, and its vocabulary — USID, ordinary/extraordinary users, temporary-able-bodied — runs through subsequent HCI accessibility literature.


## Shneiderman_2000_UniversalUsability
Shneiderman (2000) defines universal usability as having more than 90% of all households as successful weekly users of information and communications services, then presents a research agenda organized around three challenges: technology variety (the 100:1 range in processor speed, screen size, and network bandwidth), user diversity (skills, knowledge, age, disabilities, disabling conditions, literacy, culture, income), and gaps in user knowledge (the distance between what users know and what they need to know). The paper argues against the "dumbing down" and "innovation restriction" fears by invoking the curb-cut principle — that designing for extreme users expands the design space and produces innovations that benefit everyone — and distinguishes universal usability from universal access, establishing that connectivity alone does not ensure successful use. For this collection, the paper is the foundational theoretical anchor: it coined the three-challenge taxonomy that structured two decades of accessibility and usability research, established the curb-cut argument as the core justification for inclusive design investment, and set the measurable >90% threshold against which the field's progress (and persistent failure, as documented by Martins 2023 and Mack 2021) can be evaluated.


## Asakawa_2005_BlindWebBrowsing
Asakawa (2005) presents a W4A keynote combining first-person history of nonvisual web access (1994–2005) with longitudinal empirical data from 11–12 major sites, documenting how alt text coverage improved from 38% missing in 2000 to 7% missing in 2005 following Section 508 enforcement, while simultaneously demonstrating that compliance-driven improvements are frequently broken or semantically useless — including a taxonomy of four skip-navigation link failure patterns and crawl evidence that 5–6% of skip-links are broken even on accessibility-conscious sites. The paper introduces IBM aDesigner's reaching-time visualization (color gradation showing seconds to reach each page area via voice browser simulation) and argues that sighted developers must experience the blind browsing perspective through tools like Home Page Reader or aDesigner, not merely run automated compliance checkers, to produce truly usable pages. For this collection, it is the foundational historical document on screen reader / voice browser interaction with web content — establishing the compliance-vs-usability gap, the regulatory inflection point argument, and the "disability experience" rationale that motivates later user study methodologies including Lazar et al.'s frustration diary research.


## Mankoff_2010_DisabilityStudiesCriticalInquiry
Mankoff, Hayes, and Kasnitz (2010) argue that the assistive technology research community has been largely operating within the medical model of disability — treating disability as an individual deficit to fix — while disability studies has spent decades developing social, cultural, and critical frameworks that reveal richer problems and more effective research directions; the paper reviews core disability studies theory (medical/social/postmodern models, language debates, disability rights history) and presents two case studies showing how these frameworks transformed the authors' own work in autism educational technology and web accessibility evaluation. Key practical outputs include a critique of simulation-based accessibility testing as lacking the "soul" of authentic disabled experience, a recommendation for deep longitudinal participatory design with disabled individuals as equal co-researchers across all phases, and a curated reading list anchoring AT researchers in disability studies literature. For this collection, the paper provides the foundational theoretical vocabulary — ableism, nothing-about-us-without-us, social vs. medical model — that contextualizes why user-centered and participatory methods matter, why small non-representative samples (as documented in Mack_2021) are a structural problem, and why technical accessibility metrics alone are insufficient measures of whether a system actually serves disabled people.


## Porter_2013_GameAccessibilityBarriers
Porter and Kientz (ASSETS 2013) present two complementary empirical studies on mainstream video game accessibility: an online survey of 55 gamers with disabilities and semi-structured interviews with 6 game industry practitioners, finding that assistive technology incompatibility (not absent features) is the dominant real-world barrier, while multiplayer games disproportionately exclude disabled players through both technical and social mechanisms. The industry study identifies middleware (game engines like Unreal and Unity) as the highest-leverage intervention point — developers cannot feasibly build OS-level AT hooks from scratch, so standardizing accessibility in shared engines would dramatically accelerate adoption — and finds that accessibility investment requires top-down pressure from executives or legislation rather than bottom-up developer awareness. For this collection, the paper provides the empirical ground truth that Yuan_2011_GameAccessibilitySurvey's theoretical taxonomy lacks: documented barrier categories from disabled gamers themselves, industry-side explanations for why guidelines go unadopted, and a concrete structural intervention target (middleware) that frames all subsequent game accessibility work.


## Vanderheiden_1990_ThirtySomethingMillion
Vanderheiden (1990) makes the foundational argument that over 30 million Americans with disabilities (12–20% of the population) must be incorporated into mainstream human factors design rather than handled as exceptions, demonstrating through demographic data that designing to "the 95th percentile" on individual ability dimensions falsely implies near-universal usability when compound exclusion across multiple independent dimensions can exclude 40% or more of actual users. The paper presents statistical evidence that functional limitation affects 45% of those who reach age 65 and 72.5% of those who reach age 75, coins the "Temporarily Able-Bodied" framing, documents zero-marginal-cost mainstream accessibility (Apple Sticky Keys at 4KB), and shows through the elevator and curb-cut examples that accessible design routinely benefits non-disabled users while sometimes reducing costs. For this collection, it is the historical root of the entire accessible/universal design argument — the paper from which the TABs framing, the 95th percentile critique, the economic feasibility argument, and the curb-cut effect all originate, establishing the population-scale and design-philosophy foundations that every subsequent paper in this collection builds on.


## Gerling_2012_FullBodyMotionGame
Gerling et al. (2012) present two user studies with institutionalized older adults (N=15 and N=12, majority in wheelchairs or post-stroke) evaluating a Kinect-based full-body gesture system, producing a validated 8-gesture set derived from physical therapy exercises and a garden-themed game with per-player calibration of range of motion, strength, and agility. Key findings include that static gestures consistently outperform dynamic gestures for this population (overall completion 54%), that calibrated gesture recognition dramatically improves accessibility for wheelchair users, and that post-game PANAS scores showed a significant increase in positive affect (p<0.01) with no change in negative affect. For this collection, the paper provides seven concrete, empirically grounded design guidelines (Age-Inclusive Design, ROM-Adaptability, Exertion Management, Dynamic Game Difficulty, Easy Gesture Recall, Continuous Player Support, Simple Setup Routines) that translate directly into implementable requirements for any motion-based accessible game system, complementing Yuan_2011_GameAccessibilitySurvey's motor impairment taxonomy with specific calibration architecture and gesture validation data.


## Westin_2004_GameAccessibilityTerraformers
Westin (2004) documents Terraformers, a commercial real-time 3D action game developed over three years to be simultaneously playable by blind, low-vision, and sighted players, using a suite of spatial audio techniques (3D Sound Compass, Sonar, GPS) and voiced hierarchical menus as complete non-visual substitutes for the graphical interface. A post-mortem email survey of four blind paying customers found all accessibility features rated easy to use, with the main complaints being game crashes due to unstable GPU drivers and difficulty balance inconsistencies. This paper is foundational for game accessibility research: it catalysed the IGDA Game Accessibility Special Interest Group and provides the earliest detailed implementation-level case study of non-visual 3D game design using spatial audio.



## Grammenos_2009_UniversallyAccessibleGames
Grammenos, Savidis, and Stephanidis (2009) introduce the concept of universally accessible games (UA-Games) — games proactively designed to be concurrently playable by sighted, blind, and motor-impaired users without modifications — and demonstrate the concept through four case studies: UA-Chess (web chess with hierarchical scanning, voice I/O, and self-voicing), Access Invaders (profile-driven Space Invaders remake with 13 adaptation categories spanning game content and game rules), Game Over! (intentionally inaccessible educational game teaching developers what to avoid), and Terrestrial Invaders (fully-featured accessible action game). The paper presents a five-step unified design methodology adapted from universal UI design (abstract task design, polymorphic specialization, appropriateness and compatibility analysis, iterative prototyping with disabled participants) and introduces the novel Parallel Game Universes framework for enabling multiplayer sessions where each player experiences an optimally adapted version of the game connected through transition functions. For this collection, the paper is the definitive implementation reference for game accessibility: it provides concrete per-disability design parameters, a complete taxonomy of adaptation categories (Table I), the hierarchical scanning algorithm with two focus-frame states, the nonvisual navigation design, profile architecture for concurrent multi-disability play, and the PGU model for cross-disability multiplayer — directly extending and implementing the conceptual framework surveyed in Yuan_2011_GameAccessibilitySurvey.


## AguadoDelgado_2020_VideoGamesA11ySystematicReview
Aguado-Delgado et al. (2018) present a PRISMA-compliant systematic mapping study (SMS) that screens 2,805 publications and analyzes 45 primary studies on video game accessibility published 2004–2014, quantifying the research landscape by disability type (motor 30, visual 27, auditory/cognitive 20), research approach, and research questions answered. Key findings are that no existing solution guarantees universally accessible games, developers routinely ignore accessibility proposals, and zero studies address the research question of a unified software engineering methodology for accessibility — making this the largest known gap in the field as of 2014. For this collection, the paper provides the quantitative evidence base and a 45-entry appendix of primary studies with DOIs that complements Yuan_2011_GameAccessibilitySurvey's conceptual taxonomy, together forming the dual foundation for game accessibility research.


## Millar_1995_SpatialInformation
Millar (1995) summarises a programme of research with congenitally totally blind and sighted children to explain how total absence of vision disrupts spatial representation — not through cognitive deficit but through two specific informational losses: direct knowledge of external surface relations and the redundancy of reference cues that cross-modal overlap normally provides. The paper argues that body-centred spatial coding is a rational adaptation, not developmental delay, and that assistive interventions (including tactile maps, echo-location devices, and mobility training) fail when they add substitute information without explicitly integrating it with the user's existing reference frame and coding strategies. For this collection, it provides the foundational cognitive science framework for understanding blind spatial cognition — directly informing why "equivalent purpose" for spatial content (per Biggs_2025_MapEquivalentPurpose) requires matching the user's reference frame system, not just transcoding visual data into another modality.

## Biggs_2025_MapEquivalentPurpose
Biggs et al. (2025) propose the Map Equivalent-Purpose (MEP) Framework, a systematic 18-criterion evaluation template for determining whether text-based map representations serve the "equivalent purpose" required by WCAG SC 1.1.1, decomposing map purpose into three items (Generalized, Spatial Information, Spatial Relationships) and 15 equivalency criteria across Landmark, Route, and Survey spatial knowledge areas. Evaluating eight text map representations against visual baselines with 91% intercoder reliability, they find that only three approaches — Audiom interactive maps, MUD maps, and audio descriptions — achieve 100% equivalence, while commonly recommended methods like tables, turn-by-turn directions, and short alt text fail dramatically (averaging 23% equivalency), demonstrating that legacy text map methods are inadequate for WCAG compliance. For this collection, the paper provides the first operationalized definition of "equivalent purpose" for a major category of non-text web content, directly connecting to the web standards and evaluation methods research areas while highlighting that Google Maps (present on 18% of websites) produces alt text that scores 0% on Purpose and 30% on Equivalency.


## Froehlich_2025_StreetViewAI
Froehlich et al. (2025) introduce StreetReaderAI, the first accessible street view tool, which combines context-aware multimodal AI (Gemini Flash 2.0), a custom four-way GSV navigation graph, accessible keyboard controls, and conversational speech to enable blind users to virtually explore, investigate destinations, and navigate routes across Google Street View's 220+ billion images in 100+ countries. An in-person evaluation with 11 blind screen reader users demonstrated 86.3% AI accuracy on 816 factual questions, high perceived usefulness (Median 7/7), and successful completion of both point-of-interest investigation and open-world navigation tasks, while revealing key challenges in AI hallucination management, spatial orientation communication, and verbosity control. For this collection, the paper provides the state-of-the-art implementation reference for making fundamentally visual geographic tools accessible to blind users through multimodal AI, directly advancing the ability-based design principles (Wobbrock 2011), addressing the map equivalent-purpose gap (Biggs 2025), and establishing design guidelines for context-aware AI systems that combine geographic data APIs with vision-language models to produce reliable, actionable descriptions of physical environments.
