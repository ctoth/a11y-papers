# Abstract

## Original Text (Verbatim)

Accessibility research aims to understand and design technologies that are usable by people with disabilities. In this paper, we present a literature survey of accessibility research at the CHI and ASSETS conferences from 1994 to 2019. We qualitatively coded 506 papers from 2010 to 2019, and we analyzed trends across the full set of 836 papers over 26 years. We coded papers on ten dimensions, including community of focus (e.g., blind or low-vision, deaf or hard-of-hearing), study methods, contribution types, and issues addressed (e.g., digital access, physical access). We found, among other results, that 43.5% of papers focus on people who are blind or have low-vision, that participant sample sizes tend to be small (median of 13 disabled participants per paper), and that papers have been shifting from primarily technical contributions to more empirical work. We identify gaps and discuss implications for the future of accessibility research.

---

## Our Interpretation

This paper maps the entire accessibility research field as practiced at its two premier venues (CHI and ASSETS) over 26 years. It reveals substantial imbalances — blind/low-vision users receive disproportionate attention while cognitive, motor, and hearing disabilities are underserved — and documents a shift from building tools toward understanding users. For this project, it provides the essential baseline for knowing what the field has studied, what methods are standard, and where the most impactful gaps exist for future research.
