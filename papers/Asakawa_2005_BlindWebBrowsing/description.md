Asakawa (2005) presents a W4A keynote combining first-person history of nonvisual web access (1994–2005) with longitudinal empirical data from 11–12 major sites, documenting how alt text coverage improved from 38% missing in 2000 to 7% missing in 2005 following Section 508 enforcement, while simultaneously demonstrating that compliance-driven improvements are frequently broken or semantically useless — including a taxonomy of four skip-navigation link failure patterns and crawl evidence that 5–6% of skip-links are broken even on accessibility-conscious sites. The paper introduces IBM aDesigner's reaching-time visualization (color gradation showing seconds to reach each page area via voice browser simulation) and argues that sighted developers must experience the blind browsing perspective through tools like Home Page Reader or aDesigner, not merely run automated compliance checkers, to produce truly usable pages. For this collection, it is the foundational historical document on screen reader / voice browser interaction with web content — establishing the compliance-vs-usability gap, the regulatory inflection point argument, and the "disability experience" rationale that motivates later user study methodologies including Lazar et al.'s frustration diary research.
