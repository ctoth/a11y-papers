Shneiderman (2000) defines universal usability as having more than 90% of all households as successful weekly users of information and communications services, then presents a research agenda organized around three challenges: technology variety (the 100:1 range in processor speed, screen size, and network bandwidth), user diversity (skills, knowledge, age, disabilities, disabling conditions, literacy, culture, income), and gaps in user knowledge (the distance between what users know and what they need to know). The paper argues against the "dumbing down" and "innovation restriction" fears by invoking the curb-cut principle — that designing for extreme users expands the design space and produces innovations that benefit everyone — and distinguishes universal usability from universal access, establishing that connectivity alone does not ensure successful use. For this collection, the paper is the foundational theoretical anchor: it coined the three-challenge taxonomy that structured two decades of accessibility and usability research, established the curb-cut argument as the core justification for inclusive design investment, and set the measurable >90% threshold against which the field's progress (and persistent failure, as documented by Martins 2023 and Mack 2021) can be evaluated.
