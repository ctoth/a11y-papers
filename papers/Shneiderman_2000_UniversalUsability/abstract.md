# Abstract

## Original Text (Verbatim)

This article presents a research agenda based on three challenges in attaining universal usability for Web-based and other services:

- Technology variety: Supporting a broad range of hardware, software, and network access;
- User diversity: Accommodating users with different skills, knowledge, age, gender, disabilities, disabling conditions (mobility, sunlight, noise), literacy, culture, income, and so forth; and
- Gaps in user knowledge: Bridging the gap between what users know and what they need to know.

Universal usability can be defined as having more than 90% of all households as successful users of information and communications services at least once a week.

---

## Our Interpretation

The paper argues that the standard framing of the "digital divide" as a problem of access (hardware, cost, connectivity) is insufficient — achieving genuine universal usability requires solving harder HCI research problems around the diversity of technology, users, and user knowledge. Shneiderman counters the "dumbing down" fear by arguing that designing for extreme cases (disabled users, low-end hardware, novices) forces innovation that benefits all users — the curb-cut effect applied to software. For this collection, this paper is the theoretical origin point: it named the field's three core challenges and set the >90% household adoption benchmark that the accessibility research community has been working toward — and largely failing to meet — for the 25 years since publication.
