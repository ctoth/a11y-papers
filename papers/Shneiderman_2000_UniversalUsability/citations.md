## Key Citations for Follow-up

**[3] NRC/CSTB (1997) — "More than Screen Deep"**
Policy/research agenda report from the National Academy of Sciences calling for aggressive research on every-citizen interfaces. Pre-dates this paper and provides institutional backing for the research agenda. Relevant for understanding the policy landscape that framed accessibility research funding.

**[5] Kraut et al. (1996) — HomeNet field trial, CACM**
Empirical evidence that even with free equipment, free networking, training, and ongoing support, residential users had significant difficulty using Internet services. The primary empirical grounding Shneiderman uses to justify the usability (vs. access) framing. Essential companion paper.

**[6] Laux, McNally, Paciello, Vanderheiden (1996) — ASSETS '96**
Web design for people with disabilities using user-centered design. One of the earliest Web + disability design papers, authored by Gregg Vanderheiden (TRACE Center). Directly relevant to the user diversity challenge and to this collection's screen reader / web standards topic areas.

**[4] Kobsa & Stephanidis (1998) — Adaptive information access**
Adaptable and adaptive interfaces for all users including disabled and elderly. Precursor to personalization-for-accessibility work; connects to the user diversity challenge. Stephanidis went on to lead the "Design for All" / Universal Design research in Europe.

**[1] Anderson et al. (1995) Rand Corporation — Universal access to e-mail**
The landmark policy report on universal email access that preceded Shneiderman's piece. Made the case that interface understanding gaps (not just infrastructure) blocked universal access. Directly cited as establishing the need for HCI research in this space.
