## Key Citations for Follow-up

**Newell & Cairns (1987)** — "Human interface studies and the handicapped." The primary source for the curb-cut co-benefit argument and the general claim that accessible design benefits able-bodied users. Cited multiple times in this paper. Essential for the "design for disability benefits everyone" thesis.

**Kroemer (1987)** — "Engineering anthropometry" in Salvendy's Handbook of Human Factors. Cited for the critical methodological point that 95th percentile datasets often exclude people with disabilities, compounding the 95th-percentile-illusion problem. Relevant to evaluation methods and measurement validity.

**Kraus & Stoddard (1989)** — "Chartbook on disability in the United States." The primary statistical source for Vanderheiden's 30-million figure, unemployment statistics, and population breakdown data. Any paper citing disability prevalence numbers should trace back to this source.

**LaPlante (1988)** — "Data on disability from the NHIS 1983–85." Source for the multiple-impairment co-occurrence statistics (e.g., 75% of speech-impaired have other impairments). Directly relevant to any paper about assistive technology design, since solutions targeting a single disability may not serve users with co-occurring disabilities.

**Chirikos (1989)** — "Aggregate economic losses from disability in the United States." Source for the $177B economic loss figure. Relevant to economic justification papers for accessibility investment.
