# Abstract

## Original Text (Verbatim)

There are over thirty million people in the U.S. with disabilities or functional limitations (of which a major cause is aging), and this number is increasing. An examination of the role of human factors in addressing this population is presented which would include both special designs for disability/aging and the incorporation of disability/aging into mainstream human factors research and education. Statistics regarding the size and characteristics of this population are presented, including the costs of disability. Examples demonstrating the economic and commercial feasibility of incorporating disability/aging considerations in mass market designs are provided along with a discussion of the benefits to non-disabled users.

---

## Our Interpretation

The paper addresses the question of whether the 30+ million Americans with disabilities should be designed for through special-case products or through mainstream product design — and argues forcefully for the latter on demographic, economic, and pragmatic grounds. The key insight is that this is not a small population requiring exceptional accommodation but a large, growing fraction of the population (driven by aging) whose functional limitations are directly produced by poor design choices in ordinary products. For this collection, the paper establishes the foundational framing — population scale, economic feasibility, universal co-benefits — that justifies the entire accessibility research enterprise as mainstream engineering rather than niche charity work.
