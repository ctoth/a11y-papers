Vanderheiden (1990) makes the foundational argument that over 30 million Americans with disabilities (12–20% of the population) must be incorporated into mainstream human factors design rather than handled as exceptions, demonstrating through demographic data that designing to "the 95th percentile" on individual ability dimensions falsely implies near-universal usability when compound exclusion across multiple independent dimensions can exclude 40% or more of actual users. The paper presents statistical evidence that functional limitation affects 45% of those who reach age 65 and 72.5% of those who reach age 75, coins the "Temporarily Able-Bodied" framing, documents zero-marginal-cost mainstream accessibility (Apple Sticky Keys at 4KB), and shows through the elevator and curb-cut examples that accessible design routinely benefits non-disabled users while sometimes reducing costs. For this collection, it is the historical root of the entire accessible/universal design argument — the paper from which the TABs framing, the 95th percentile critique, the economic feasibility argument, and the curb-cut effect all originate, establishing the population-scale and design-philosophy foundations that every subsequent paper in this collection builds on.
