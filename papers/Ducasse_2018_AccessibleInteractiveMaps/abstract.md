# Abstract

## Original Text (Verbatim)

In this chapter, we review interactive map prototypes that have been designed for visually impaired users. We classified existing interactive maps into two categories: Digital Interactive Maps (DIMs) that are displayed on a flat surface such as a screen, and Hybrid Interactive Maps (HIMs) that include both a digital and a physical display. In each family, we identified several subcategories depending on the technology being used. We compared the categories and subcategories according to cost, usability, and functionality. Then, because evaluation is essential in order to verify that devices are usable, we reviewed a number of studies showing that they can support spatial learning for visually impaired users. In the following section, we discussed the similarities and differences between maps and other types of graphics (such as bar charts, organigrams, etc.) Finally, we identified new technologies and methods that could improve the accessibility of graphics for visually impaired users (e.g. shape-changing interfaces and 3D printing).

---

## Our Interpretation

This book chapter tackles the problem that accessible interactive maps have been developed using wildly different technologies over three decades with no agreed-upon classification or terminology. The authors create a comprehensive two-level taxonomy (Digital vs. Hybrid, with technology-based subcategories) and compare ~60 prototypes across content capability, readability, interactivity, and spatial cognition support. The result is the definitive reference framework for anyone designing or evaluating non-visual map access systems, establishing that hybrid approaches with physical tactile displays outperform purely digital maps for spatial learning, while identifying refreshable displays and 3D printing as the most promising but still immature future directions.
