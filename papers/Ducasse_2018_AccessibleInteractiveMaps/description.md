Ducasse, Brock, and Jouffrais (2018) present a comprehensive classification and review of three decades of interactive map prototypes for visually impaired users, organizing ~60 systems into two families -- Digital Interactive Maps (purely screen-based) and Hybrid Interactive Maps (combining digital and physical displays) -- with subcategories by input device type and physical display technology, and comparing them across five dimensions: map content, readability, haptic reference frame, interactivity, and spatial cognition support. Key findings include that Hybrid Interactive Maps (especially Interactive Raised-Line Maps) provide superior tactile reference frames and spatial learning outcomes compared to purely digital maps, that two-handed exploration significantly outperforms single-point-of-contact interaction, and that refreshable tactile displays are theoretically ideal but remain too expensive and low-resolution for practical deployment.
