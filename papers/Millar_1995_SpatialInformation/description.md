Millar (1995) summarises a programme of research with congenitally totally blind and sighted children to explain how total absence of vision disrupts spatial representation — not through cognitive deficit but through two specific informational losses: direct knowledge of external surface relations and the redundancy of reference cues that cross-modal overlap normally provides. The paper argues that body-centred spatial coding is a rational adaptation, not developmental delay, and that assistive interventions (including tactile maps, echo-location devices, and mobility training) fail when they add substitute information without explicitly integrating it with the user's existing reference frame and coding strategies. For this collection, it provides the foundational cognitive science framework for understanding blind spatial cognition — directly informing why "equivalent purpose" for spatial content (per Biggs_2025_MapEquivalentPurpose) requires matching the user's reference frame system, not just transcoding visual data into another modality.
