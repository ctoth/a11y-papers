Newell and Gregor argue that "Universal Design" and "Design for All" set goals that are unachievable for most HCI products — accessibility for one disability group often conflicts with accessibility for another, and claiming universal coverage discourages designers from attempting inclusive work at all. They propose "User Sensitive Inclusive Design" (USID) as an extension of User Centred Design that acknowledges the extraordinary breadth of disabled user functionality, specifies seven methodological challenges that distinguish inclusive UCD from mainstream UCD, and substitutes "sensitive" for "centred" and "inclusive" for "universal" to reflect what is actually achievable. This paper is the canonical theoretical foundation for framing accessibility research as a distinct paradigm rather than a checklist, and its vocabulary — USID, ordinary/extraordinary users, temporary-able-bodied — runs through subsequent HCI accessibility literature.
