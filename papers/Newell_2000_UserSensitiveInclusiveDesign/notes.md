# "User Sensitive Inclusive Design" — in Search of a New Paradigm

**Authors:** Alan F. Newell, Peter Gregor
**Year:** 2000
**Venue:** Proceedings of the 2000 Conference on Universal Usability (CUU '00), ACM, Arlington VA, pp. 39–44
**DOI:** 10.1145/355460.355470

## One-Sentence Summary

Argues that "Universal Design" / "Design for All" sets an unachievable goal for most HCI contexts, and proposes "User Sensitive Inclusive Design" — an extension of User Centred Design that is explicitly sensitive to the wide functional variability of disabled user groups rather than claiming universal coverage.

## Problem Addressed

Existing design-for-all frameworks (Universal Design, Design for All, Accessible Design, Inclusive Design) adopt an "equitable use by any group" premise that is, in practice, impossible for most products. The paper argues this over-reach creates two harms: it discourages designers from attempting inclusive design at all ("excellence as enemy of the good"), and it fails to acknowledge genuine conflicts between the accessibility needs of different disability groups. Standard User Centred Design methodology also does not adequately handle the special ethical and logistical challenges of recruiting and involving disabled participants.

## Key Contributions

- Introduces the term and concept "User Sensitive Inclusive Design" (USID) as a replacement for "User Centred Design" when the user group includes disabled people.
- Provides a seven-point list of methodological challenges UCD must address to handle disabled user groups (variety, recruitment difficulty, inter-disability conflicts, temporary vs. permanent disability trade-offs, inapplicable universal claims, user-group specification, component-based accessibility).
- Argues that "sensitive" replaces "centred" because the range of user functionality can be so wide that a small representative sample is impossible — only sensitivity to the entire distribution is feasible.
- Argues "inclusive" replaces "universal" because inclusivity is achievable; full universality usually is not.
- Critiques Participatory Action Research (PAR) in the HCI context: disabled users should be "involved in" the process but should not control research agendas, evaluation methodology choice, or question formulation.
- Documents the Dundee model: disabled consultants as full "test pilot" team members alongside user panels and formal case studies.
- Notes that blue-sky / long-term research must sometimes deliberately ignore articulated user needs and clinician opinion, citing predictive text for dyslexia (eventually accepted after years of resistance) and CHAT social conversation AAC system (initially rejected by therapists, now widely adopted).

## Methodology

Position/argument paper grounded in the authors' practice at the University of Dundee. No empirical study; draws on Newell's prior published work (1984–1998) and standard HCI/UCD literature. Surveys existing Universal Design frameworks (INCLUDE/EU, NC State Centre for Universal Design, W3C WAI) and identifies their limitations.

## Implementation Details

The paper identifies these concrete modifications UCD must make to become USID:

1. **User variability** — account for a much greater variety of user characteristics and functionality than mainstream UCD.
2. **Recruitment** — address difficulty finding and recruiting representative users (especially AAC users, users with cognitive or communication dysfunction).
3. **Inter-disability conflicts** — explicitly surface conflicts between accessibility requirements for different disability types (e.g., floor texture helps blind users, hinders wheelchair users).
4. **Temporary able-bodied conflicts** — acknowledge tensions between solutions for severe disability and ease-of-use for less-disabled or temporarily-disabled users.
5. **Scope exclusions** — recognise situations where universal access is inappropriate by definition (e.g., blind drivers), not as failure but as honest scoping.
6. **User-group specification** — specify exactly the functional characteristics of the target user group rather than claiming "all users".
7. **Component-based accessibility** — allow accessibility to be provided via additional components (e.g., overlay keyboards, AT devices) rather than requiring it to be built into the core product.

**Clinician integration:** Clinicians must be employed on the research team (not consulted externally) to develop a research ethos; ad-hoc clinical consultation risks substituting expert opinion for user observation.

**Blue-sky research stance:** Long-term research teams must be empowered to pursue directions users and clinicians currently reject — the CHAT example shows a multi-year lag between innovation and clinical acceptance.

## Results Summary

No empirical results. The paper's "result" is the conceptual reframing: USID as a more honest, more achievable goal than Universal Design, and a specific list of the challenges that distinguish it from mainstream UCD.

## Limitations

- Authors acknowledge this paper exclusively addresses people with disabilities, noting the framework applies "mutatis mutandis" to other minority groups.
- No empirical validation of USID as a methodology — it is a proposal, not a tested framework.
- The paper does not provide concrete methodology steps, only the list of challenges to address and the high-level principles. The methodology itself is called for as future collaborative work.
- The PAR critique is brief and may understate the valid role of participatory co-design (the paper's framing risks being read as dismissive of disability rights perspectives).

## Testable Properties

This is a conceptual paper; there are no algorithms or parameters. However, the framework implies evaluable properties:

- Any USID process should be able to name the specific user group (functional characteristics) it targets — if it claims "all users," it violates the USID principle.
- An accessibility solution that improves access for group A while degrading it for group B must be flagged as a conflict, not counted as a success.
- Research involving AAC or cognitively impaired users should document whether clinicians are team members (not just consultants).
- Long-term research programs should document decisions made against current user/clinician preference with rationale, to enable later review.

## Relevance to Project

This paper is the theoretical foundation for inclusive/accessible design as a research paradigm. It is the canonical source for the argument that "Universal Design" is inadequate for HCI contexts involving disabled users. Any project evaluating accessibility methodologies, research ethics with disabled participants, or the scope and limits of WCAG/WAI standards should engage with this paper. It provides the vocabulary (USID, "user sensitive", "inclusive vs. universal") that appears throughout subsequent literature.
