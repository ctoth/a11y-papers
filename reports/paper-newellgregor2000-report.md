## Usefulness Rating: **High**

### What it provides

- The canonical theoretical justification for why accessibility design needs a distinct research paradigm, not just applied UCD principles — cited broadly throughout HCI accessibility literature.
- Seven concrete methodological challenges that distinguish inclusive UCD from mainstream UCD: user variability, recruitment difficulty, inter-disability conflicts, temporary-vs-permanent disability trade-offs, scope exclusions, user-group specification, and component-based accessibility.
- The vocabulary distinction "inclusive" (achievable) vs. "universal" (usually not) and "sensitive" vs. "centred" — used by subsequent papers including Wobbrock 2011.
- A documented research practice model from Dundee: disabled consultants as team "test pilots" plus user panels, with clinicians employed on the team rather than consulted externally.
- An argument for blue-sky research that can deliberately ignore current user and clinician preferences — supported by two concrete cases (predictive text for dyslexia, CHAT AAC) where Dundee research was correct and clinical resistance delayed adoption by years.

### Actionable next steps

- Use USID's seven-challenge list as an evaluation checklist when reviewing any study that claims to include disabled participants: does it address recruitment, inter-disability conflict, and user-group specification?
- Retrieve Newell (1995) "Extra-ordinary Human Computer Operation" — this paper cannot be fully understood without that predecessor, and several subsequent papers in the collection reference the ordinary/extraordinary framing without citing back to it.
- Note the ordinary/extraordinary framing (space engineers as temporarily disabled; gloves as situational impairment; narrow mobile phone keyboard as universal handicap) as a precursor to situational impairment work that feeds into modern mobile accessibility research.

### Skip if

- The project needs empirical methods, evaluation instruments, or quantitative data — this paper contains none of those. It is entirely theoretical/argumentative.
