## Usefulness to This Project

**Rating:** High — foundational

**What it provides:**
- The historical origin of the "design for the mainstream, not as exceptions" argument that the entire collection builds on (predates ADA, WCAG, and all other papers in collection by at least a decade)
- The 95th percentile illusion: a mathematically demonstrable argument for why standard human factors design methodology structurally excludes 12–20% of the population
- The "Temporarily Able-Bodied" (TABs) framing — the conceptual vocabulary that reframes disability as a universal lifecycle phenomenon rather than a minority identity
- Concrete zero-marginal-cost accessibility examples (Apple Sticky Keys 4KB, Mouse Keys 4KB, CloseView 20KB) that refute "too expensive" objections with 1990 data
- Quantitative population data: >30M Americans, 45% functional limitation at 65+, 72.5% at 75+
- The curb-cut effect: accessible design benefits non-disabled users; elevator advance-signal example shows it can also reduce cost

**Actionable for this collection:**
- Any paper making a claim about disability prevalence should cite Vanderheiden 1990 as the historical baseline (noting that the 30M figure was for 1990 and has grown substantially)
- The 95th percentile illusion is a testable claim worth connecting to evaluation methodology papers (Pool_2023_AccessibilityMetatesting, Martins_2023_LargeScaleWebA11y)
- The TABs concept connects forward to Wobbrock_2011_AbilityBasedDesign's "ability space" framing and backward to nothing in our collection that predates it

**Limitations to note:**
- Pre-Web paper; all examples are hardware products and operating system software
- No original data; all statistics from secondary sources (1979–1989 surveys)
- Demographic figures are 1990 US-specific; do not generalize directly to current global figures
